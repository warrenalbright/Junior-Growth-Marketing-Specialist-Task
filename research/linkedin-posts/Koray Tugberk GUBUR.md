Can LLM-manipulating listicles and brand mentions help local businesses rank better?

Yes.

And I intentionally use the word “manipulating,” not “optimizing.”

Because in this context, we are not only improving a page.

We are artificially changing document statistics across the web to create a new consensus around a brand, service provider, and entity.

One of the law agency teams I coach is actively applying this.

They also recently hired new teammates from our community, which makes the project even more meaningful for me.

The method is based on what we call “external topical maps.”

In simple terms:

Multiple domains create a connected document network.

That network repeats, aligns, and reinforces the same attributes, values, claims, services, and entity relationships for a specific local business.

From an external publication, you can technically get 3 things:

A mention
A claim
A link

In this case, the main focus is not only links.

It is mentions and claims.

Because mentions and claims help form consensus.

And consensus helps search engines and LLMs understand who the brand is, what it does, where it operates, and why it should be trusted.

This law firm reached record leads in May, mainly due to stronger local rankings.

It is also the same local law firm website I previously shared in the context of visual semantics, which reached rankings with an estimated query value of more than $500,000 in the accident and personal injury space.

But there is another important principle here.

The project is also building a strong informational section around legal terms, law-related entities, and legal processes.

For location-agnostic queries, do not only look at national rankings.

For example, if you rank for “types of damages,” the real question is not only:

“Do we rank nationally?”

The better question is:

“Are we getting impressions and clicks from the main city we actually serve?”

Because for a local law firm, an informational page is not successful only when it ranks broadly.

It is successful when it strengthens local demand, local impressions, and local entity relevance.

That is how informational SEO supports local SEO.

That is how external topical maps support brand consensus.

And that is how mentions, claims, and document networks can influence both search engines and LLMs.

Good news for SEOs: Google is bringing Generative AI Performance reporting to Google Search Console.

At first, it will mainly help us track AI appearances and impressions, but this is already a meaningful step forward.

Why?

Because we can finally start separating traditional web-result impressions from AI-result impressions more clearly.

For Holistic SEOs, this opens many new analytical layers:

Compare crawl stats and specific user agents with AI appearances.

Compare sentence structures, content freshness, and source responsiveness against AI visibility.

Compare AI performance with the average position of commercial queries.

Compare free-tool query segments with AI appearances to understand whether helpful utility-based assets support broader brand and topical relevance.

For now, the report may not feel fully satisfying because Google is mostly giving impression-level data.

But even with this limitation, there are many new metrics, patterns, and signals that a Holistic SEO can create from it.

AI visibility is not only about being mentioned.

It is about understanding why a source is selected, when it is selected, and which semantic, technical, and behavioral signals support that selection.

Google has updated its guidelines for AI Search by making one thing clearer:

AEO, GEO, SXO, or whatever people choose to call it, is technically the same discipline as SEO.

If your web document cannot rank, your passages cannot rank.

If your passages cannot rank, generated AI answers are less likely to cite you.

The “query fan-out” definitions in the documentation also show that this is simply query augmentation, as we have been saying from day one.

Google’s Search Relations team is now trying to clarify these concepts. But in my opinion, they gave hype-marketers this opportunity in the first place by using unclear and irresponsible terminology.

RAG is basically an aggregated index curator that generates an answer for a specific, explicit question-query.

When the query is implicit or incomplete, augmentation becomes more diverse, and the number of retrieved documents increases.

PageRank, Topical Authority, and consensus are always part of RAG in terms of result filtering, trust, and source selection.

There is one research paper I suggest you read: “Corroboration of Web Answers.”

It helped inspire the logic behind featured snippets. If you check the sections on “prominence of an answer” and “answer scoring,” you can see that the same principles still reflect what we discuss today.

With that said, many hype-marketers are trying to push AEO, GEO, and similar terms as separate services.

In reality, good SEO, especially Semantic SEO, already communicates with LLMs in the best possible way.

You do not need a completely separate discipline only for LLMs.

In some cases, to impact consensus or convince LLMs, we can create specific domains. I usually call them “wasteful domains,” because we do not create these web documents to rank for a query directly, but to influence consensus as an objective third-party source.

In other cases, we can open different domains with EMD or PMD characteristics to impact the rankings of certain terms. We call these “extension domains.”

But none of these are beyond SEO.

They are all just different SEO implementations.

Since day one, we have defended this idea.
